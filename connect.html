Putting "The Enable It - Model It - Embrace It" at work

How can we ensure that the young generations of marginalized groups' imagined future is not biased by the effects of the racist circumstances that they grow up in?
A question inspired by Professor Ruha Benjamin.

Evidence indicates that STEM employment has grown 79% (9.7 million to 17.3 million) since 1990 and computer jobs have seen an astounding increase of 338% over the same period. While there has been a significant increase in the number of jobs in those fields, Black and Hispanic workers continue to be underrepresented.

For example, Black people and Hispanic people comprise 11% and 16% of the U.S. workforce respectively but represent only 9% and 7% of the STEM workers, respectively. In addition to that, among employed adults with a bachelorâ€™s degree or higher, Blacks are just 7% and Hispanics are 6% of the STEM workforce.

These numbers show how marginalized groups are kept out of STEM fields and provide an image of a system that constantly oppresses the socio-economic development of marginalized groups as young people from these groups grow up not seeing themselves in those fields, which reduces their motivation to pursue careers in STEM.

Even though the underrepresentation of marginalized groups in STEM affects the motivation of young people in committing to these fields, research by the National Endowment for the Arts showed that students from underrepresented groups in terms of STEAM (STEM and art) are more actively engaged so that they are more likely to be successful.

Therefore, as the Citizen Science Lab(a lab created through professor Ruha Benjamin's Class), we saw this as an opportunity to embark on a mission to empower marginalized groups kept out of STEAM fields by offering tools and funding to be researchers. Through leading initiatives that fund community projects and introduce new and productive STEAM skills, we hope to partner with African American, Hispanic, and Native-American communities to give the scientific agency back to local communities and promote interpersonal connections among the members of those communities.

The above case study shows the possibility of using the "The Enable It - Model It - Embrace It" to solve the world's alarming problems.
